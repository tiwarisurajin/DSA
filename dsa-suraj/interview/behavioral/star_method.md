# Behavioral Interviews — STAR Method

## The structure

- **Situation** — brief context, 1-2 sentences. Don't over-explain the backstory.
- **Task** — what were you specifically responsible for (not the team — you).
- **Action** — what YOU did, step by step. This is the longest section. Use "I," not "we," even on team projects — the interviewer needs to isolate your individual contribution.
- **Result** — the outcome, ideally with a concrete number or measurable change. If there wasn't a clean win, say what you learned instead — a well-articulated failure with a genuine lesson is a stronger answer than a vague, unverifiable success.

## Build a story bank, not answers to specific questions

Behavioral questions are drawn from a small set of underlying themes. Instead of memorizing an answer per possible question, prepare 5–7 real stories from your own experience, each mapped to multiple themes:

| Theme | Questions it covers |
|---|---|
| Conflict / disagreement | "Tell me about a disagreement with a teammate," "time you pushed back on a decision" |
| Failure / mistake | "Tell me about a time you failed," "biggest mistake" |
| Ownership under ambiguity | "Time you had to figure something out with no clear direction" |
| Learning something hard, fast | "Time you had to learn a new technology quickly" |
| Prioritization under pressure | "Time you had competing deadlines" |

## For a self-taught / career-transition background specifically

Framing that tends to land well:
- Lead with **what you built and why**, not with the credential gap. "I built Sarvagya, a multi-agent content orchestration system" is a stronger opener than "I don't have a CS degree, but..."
- If asked directly about the non-traditional path, answer it in one or two sentences and pivot back to concrete work — dwelling on it reads as insecurity even when the interviewer's question was neutral.
- Have one story ready about a genuinely hard technical decision you made and had to defend or later reverse (e.g. an architecture choice) — this is usually a stronger "ownership" story than a workplace-conflict story, precisely because you can speak to the technical reasoning in depth.

## Common mistakes

- Rambling without hitting Result — practicing out loud with a timer (aim for 90 seconds per story) catches this fast.
- Using "we" throughout an "individual contribution" question, leaving the interviewer unable to tell what you specifically did.
- Choosing a story where the lesson is generic ("I learned communication is important") instead of specific ("I learned to send a written summary after verbal syncs because two team members had acted on different understandings of the same conversation").
